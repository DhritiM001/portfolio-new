Fast-Tracking Innovation: How Pretotyping, XYZ Hypothesis, and Hypozooming Work Together

November 18th, 2024

Bringing innovative ideas to life is exciting, but it’s also fraught with uncertainty. How do you know if an idea will work? How can you validate it without wasting months (or years) of effort and resources? Alberto Savoia, a leading voice in innovation strategy, offers three practical tools, pretotyping, XYZ hypothesis, and hypozooming, to reduce risk, optimize decision-making, and increase the chances of success.


Together, these concepts provide a roadmap for testing ideas and making informed decisions, whether you’re building a product, refining a service, or solving a complex problem. Let’s break down how they work and why they matter.


What Is Pretotyping?

Pretotyping is the art of “faking it before making it.” It focuses on testing the core assumptions of an idea with minimal resources. Instead of building a fully functional product, you create a lightweight version, or even just a representation, that can provide actionable feedback.


For example, before building the hardware for a smart fitness tracker, you might ask potential users to interact with a cardboard prototype or a simple app that simulates the product’s features. This helps you answer critical questions: Do people understand the concept? Are they excited about it? Would they actually use it?


Pretotyping ensures you’re not wasting time building something nobody wants. By identifying flaws or refining ideas early, you save both time and money while focusing on what truly resonates with your audience.


The XYZ Hypothesis: Structuring Assumptions

Pretotyping works best when paired with a strong foundation of hypotheses. That’s where the XYZ hypothesis comes in. It’s a framework that structures assumptions into testable statements:


“If we do X, then Y will happen because of Z.”


This formula clarifies what you’re testing (X), the expected outcome (Y), and the rationale behind it (Z). For example:


If we advertise a 7-day free trial on social media (X), then sign-ups will increase by 20% (Y) because users want to try before they commit (Z).


This approach transforms vague ideas into measurable experiments. It also ensures you’re focused on the why behind user behavior, helping you make decisions backed by data rather than assumptions.


Hypozooming: Balancing Focus and Strategy

While pretotyping and the XYZ hypothesis help with testing ideas, hypozooming ensures you’re looking at the right ideas in the first place. Hypozooming involves alternating between two perspectives:


1. Zooming out to see the bigger picture, identify broad opportunities, and clarify overarching goals.


2. Zooming in to test the fine details, adjust small variables, and refine specific aspects of your strategy.


For instance, let’s say you’re designing a new e-commerce platform. Zooming out helps you align the platform’s development with market trends and long-term user needs. Zooming in ensures the shopping cart functionality works seamlessly and user feedback is implemented effectively.


Hypozooming keeps you agile, ensuring that each step you take contributes to both short-term progress and long-term success.


How These Tools Work Together

Each of these tools is powerful on its own, but their real strength lies in how they complement each other:


1. Pretotyping enables quick and cost-effective experimentation.


2. The XYZ hypothesis clarifies what to test and what success looks like.


3. Hypozooming keeps you aligned with broader objectives while focusing on actionable details.


For example, let’s say you’re working on an AI-driven chatbot for customer service. Using pretotyping, you could create a low-cost simulation of the chatbot’s functionality, such as a simple script. The XYZ hypothesis would help you test whether users prefer automated responses to live agents and why. Hypozooming would ensure the chatbot aligns with the company’s long-term strategy while refining the user experience with feedback from small-scale trials.


Applying These Concepts to My Work

In my own experience, these concepts have reshaped how I approach problem-solving and innovation. While working on dashboard creation at Ernst & Young, I often had to test multiple designs and functionalities to meet client needs. By pretotyping dashboards (using mockups and simplified data visualizations), I could gauge client interest without committing to a full build.


The XYZ hypothesis guided my experiments, helping me identify which dashboard elements resonated with clients and why. For example:


If we use real-time inventory data on the main page (X), then managers will prefer it over static reports (Y) because they value immediate decision-making tools (Z).


Finally, hypozooming allowed me to balance client-specific requests with broader business goals, ensuring that each dashboard not only met individual needs but also contributed to long-term process improvements.


Conclusion: Innovate Smarter, Not Harder

Pretotyping, XYZ hypothesis, and hypozooming are invaluable tools for anyone looking to innovate efficiently. Whether you’re building a new product, refining an existing process, or exploring a new market, these methods can help you validate ideas quickly, minimize risk, and align actions with strategic goals.


The next time you face an uncertain project, remember: test fast, stay focused, and always keep the bigger picture in mind.
